# Live in Abundance — Video Series
### Full Plan + Scripts for All 8 Episodes (8+ minutes each)

---

## Approach

Each video is built around **one genuine passage of Scripture**, not a promise of
instant miracles. The structure deliberately avoids the manipulative tactics of the
"stop looking for a job" source video — no manufactured urgency, no "type this in the
comments" bait. Instead: a steady, repeatable format people can trust week after week.

**Episode format (8–9 minutes):**
1. **Hook** (30 sec)
2. **The text** — read slowly, with brief explanation
3. **The story** — the biblical episode behind it
4. **The balance** — work and trust together, not one instead of the other
5. **A time of reflection** — a slower, meditative passage to extend the episode
6. **Prayer** — drawn from the companion book
7. **Soft close** — invite continued viewing, no manipulation

## Episode 1: "The Shepherd Who Cares"
### Script (approx. 8:30)

**[0:00–0:30] Hook**

> There's a prayer said at deathbeds and at first communions, at hospital bedsides
> and at funerals. It's about the simplest thing there is: that we won't be left
> hungry, and we won't be left alone. It's Psalm 23. Today, just this — no promises
> of instant wealth. Just what it actually says.

**[0:30–2:00] The text**

*(text on screen, read slowly)*

> The Lord is my shepherd; I shall not want. He maketh me to lie down in green
> pastures: he leadeth me beside the still waters. He restoreth my soul...

Notice what it doesn't say. It doesn't say "I will never be poor" or "I'll get
everything I want." It talks about a shepherd and a sheep. And a shepherd doesn't
just throw down some food and walk away — he **leads**, day after day, to where the
food and water actually are.

**[2:00–4:00] The story**

David didn't write this from a palace. He wrote it remembering years as a literal
shepherd boy — and years running from Saul through the wilderness, with no roof over
his head, sleeping in caves. These aren't the words of a man who has everything.
They're the words of a man who learned, through hard experience, that God does not
abandon — even when the circumstances look terrifying. Look at 1 Samuel 22–24: David
hiding in the cave of Adullam, four hundred men in debt and distress gathering around
him, no income, no security — and yet, years later, this is the psalm he writes.

**[4:00–6:00] The balance**

It would be easy to want this psalm to say: "don't look for work, just believe, and
the money will come." But it's not saying that. It's saying that even when we do
everything we can — search, work, knock on doors — we are not alone. The Shepherd
walks alongside. That doesn't cancel our effort. It changes the spirit in which we
carry it out — without panic, because the Shepherd is near. Think of verse 5: "thou
preparest a table before me in the presence of mine enemies." Not in the absence of
trouble — in the middle of it. The table is prepared *while* the enemies are still
watching. That's what trust in providence looks like in real life: not the absence
of difficulty, but a steadiness inside the difficulty.

**[6:00–7:00] A moment of reflection**

Sit with verse 4 for a moment: "though I walk through the valley of the shadow of
death, I will fear no evil: for thou art with me." Not "I will feel no fear" — but "I
will fear no evil," because fear itself is being answered by a presence, not by a
guarantee that nothing bad will happen. That distinction matters. This psalm was never
a promise that hardship would be avoided. It's a promise that hardship would be
walked through, not walked through alone.

**[7:00–8:00] Prayer**

*(slow, with subtitles)*

> Lord God, my Shepherd, in whom I shall lack nothing. You fed Elijah by a raven, and
> the widow by flour that did not fail, and Peter by a catch beyond his night's labor.
> Do not take away my hands or my diligence, but bless the work of them. Give me this
> day my daily bread, and do not let me be led into anxiety over tomorrow. Amen.

**[8:00–8:30] Soft close**

> If this psalm moved something in you, stay with the series — seven more episodes
> ahead, each built around one genuine passage of Scripture. No 24-hour promises. Just
> words worth trusting, one week at a time.

## Episode 2: "Those Who Seek the Lord Lack Nothing"
### Script (approx. 8:15)

**[0:00–0:30] Hook**

> Young lions — the most powerful hunters in the wild — sometimes go hungry. That's
> in the Bible. And right next to it is a promise about people who seek God. Today
> we look at both halves of that verse, together.

**[0:30–2:00] The text**

> O taste and see that the Lord is good: blessed is the man that trusteth in him. O
> fear the Lord, ye his saints: for there is no want to them that fear him. The young
> lions do lack, and suffer hunger: but they that seek the Lord shall not want any
> good thing.

The comparison is deliberate. Lions represent raw strength — the world's picture of
self-sufficiency. And even they go hungry sometimes. Strength alone isn't the
guarantee. Something else is.

**[2:00–4:00] The story**

This psalm — 34 — has a heading: "A Psalm of David, when he changed his behaviour
before Abimelech; who drove him away, and he departed." Read 1 Samuel 21:10–15. David
is fleeing Saul, alone, terrified, and ends up pretending to be insane in front of a
Philistine king just to survive. He drools on his beard, scratches at doors like a
madman — an image about as far from "successful and secure" as you can get. That is
the man who then writes: "I sought the Lord, and he heard me, and delivered me from
all my fears" (v. 4). The confidence in this psalm was forged in humiliation, not in
comfort.

**[4:00–6:00] The balance**

This is not a psalm that says "seeking the Lord" is a substitute for effort — the
whole psalm is about a man in an active, desperate, undignified survival situation.
"Seeking the Lord" here doesn't mean sitting still. It means: in the middle of doing
what you have to do, keeping your orientation toward God rather than toward panic.
David didn't stop trying to escape. He kept moving, kept scheming, kept surviving —
and underneath all of it, he was seeking God's help, not just his own cleverness.

**[6:00–7:00] A moment of reflection**

Verse 6 says: "This poor man cried, and the Lord heard him, and saved him out of all
his troubles." Not "before" his troubles. Not instead of his troubles. Out of them.
Sit with that difference for a moment — it reframes what this promise is actually for.

**[7:00–8:00] Prayer**

> O taste and see that the Lord is good, blessed is the one who trusts in Him. Lord,
> in the middle of my own scrambling and striving, keep my eyes turned toward You
> and not only toward my own fear. Let me lack no good thing, not because I am
> strong, but because You are faithful. Amen.

**[8:00–8:15] Soft close**

> Next time: Isaac, a famine, and a harvest that made no economic sense — but he
> still had to sow the seed first.

## Episode 3: "The Sower: Labor and Blessing Together"
### Script (approx. 8:20)

**[0:00–0:30] Hook**

> A hundredfold harvest — in the middle of a famine. That's what happened to Isaac.
> But here's the detail people skip past: before any of that, he still had to go out
> and sow the seed.

**[0:30–1:30] The text**

> Then Isaac sowed in that land, and received in the same year an hundredfold: and
> the Lord blessed him.

One sentence. Easy to read past it. But look at the order: he sowed, and he received.
Not: he waited, and he received.

**[1:30–4:00] The story**

Read the fuller context in Genesis 26. There's a famine in the land — the same kind
of famine that sent Isaac's father Abraham down to Egypt a generation earlier. God
tells Isaac not to go down to Egypt this time, but to stay in Gerar, in the land of
the Philistines. That's a strange, almost illogical command: stay in a famine-struck
land instead of leaving for where the food is. And what does Isaac do with that
instruction? He sows. In a famine. In someone else's territory, surrounded by people
who would soon envy him so much they'd stop up his wells (v. 14–15). The hundredfold
harvest didn't arrive instead of the difficult circumstances — it arrived inside
them, as a direct result of an act of both faith and labor happening at the same
time.

**[4:00–6:00] The balance**

This is maybe the clearest example in the whole Old Testament of the two things this
series keeps insisting on holding together. Isaac had a word from God. He also had a
plow. Neither one on its own tells the whole story. If Isaac had only had the word and
not sown anything, there would have been no harvest to bless. If he'd only sown
without the word directing him to stay, he might have made the wrong decision
entirely and gone down to Egypt like his father did. Guidance and labor, together —
that's the pattern.

**[6:00–7:00] A moment of reflection**

Consider what "a hundredfold" would have meant to Isaac's neighbors, watching a man
farm through a famine and somehow come out ahead. Verse 16 says the Philistines told
him to leave: "for thou art much mightier than we." The blessing wasn't private —
it was visible enough to provoke both envy and eventual respect (see v. 26–31, where
Abimelech comes seeking peace with him). Abundance that's rooted in patient,
God-guided labor tends to become undeniable over time, even to skeptics.

**[7:00–8:00] Prayer**

> Lord, teach me to sow even when the ground looks unpromising and the season looks
> wrong. Give me a word to stand on, the way Isaac had Your word to stay. And give me
> the hands to do the sowing itself — not waiting for the harvest to arrive without
> the seed going into the ground first. Bless the work of my hands, that it not be in
> vain. Amen.

**[8:00–8:20] Soft close**

> Next episode: an entire nation, in a desert, with no farms and no markets — fed
> every single morning for forty years. What manna actually teaches about provision.

## Episode 4: "Bread from Heaven: Manna in the Wilderness"
### Script (approx. 8:40)

**[0:00–0:30] Hook**

> Two million people, give or take, in a desert with no crops, no livestock market,
> no supply chain of any kind — fed, every single morning, for forty years. This
> episode is about the strangest grocery story in the Bible.

**[0:30–2:00] The text**

> Then said the Lord unto Moses, Behold, I will rain bread from heaven for you; and
> the people shall go out and gather a certain rate every day... And the children of
> Israel did eat manna forty years, until they came to a land inhabited.

Forty years. Not forty days. Every single morning, without fail, for four decades.

**[2:00–4:30] The story**

Read Exodus 16 in full and the details get more interesting, not less. The people had
just left Egypt — where at least there was food, even as slaves (v. 3, they actually
say they miss the "flesh pots" of Egypt). They're in a wilderness with nothing. And
God's instructions are oddly specific: gather only what you need for the day (v. 4).
Try to hoard extra, and it breeds worms and stinks by morning (v. 20) — except on the
day before the Sabbath, when a double portion keeps fine (v. 22–24). This wasn't
random abundance. It was abundance with a built-in lesson about daily dependence
rather than stockpiling out of fear. The people who tried to hoard against the rules
learned the hard way that the provision worked differently than their instincts told
them to expect.

**[4:30–6:00] The balance**

Even here, in the most miraculous provision story in the Old Testament, there was
still something required of the people: they had to go out and gather it (v. 4, "the
people shall go out and gather"). The bread fell from heaven, but nobody's tent
filled up automatically while they stayed inside. Every single day, for forty years,
someone had to walk out and pick it up off the ground. Miraculous provision and
personal effort, again, side by side — not substitutes for each other.

**[6:00–7:30] A moment of reflection**

There's something worth sitting with in verse 4's phrase "a certain rate every day" —
literally, "the portion of a day in its day." Not a year's supply. Not even a week's.
A day's worth, for that day. It's the same shape as the line in the Lord's Prayer:
"give us this day our daily bread" — not "give us a decade of security so we never
have to ask again." There's something almost uncomfortable about that, if you're
someone who wants guarantees stretching far into the future. But it's also, maybe,
a kind of freedom: you're not required to solve tomorrow today.

**[7:30–8:20] Prayer**

> Lord, You fed a whole nation one day at a time, in a place with no way to farm and
> no way to buy. Teach me to trust You the same way — not demanding to see the whole
> year's provision laid out in advance, but willing to go out, gather what today
> requires, and trust that tomorrow will have its own portion when tomorrow comes.
> Give us this day our daily bread. Amen.

**[8:20–8:40] Soft close**

> Next time: a widow with one handful of flour left before she expected to die — and
> what happened when she gave the last of it away first.

## Episode 5: "The Flour That Did Not Run Out"
### Script (approx. 8:30)

**[0:00–0:30] Hook**

> A widow with enough flour and oil for exactly one last meal before she and her son
> expected to starve to death. A prophet asks her to give it away first. This is one
> of the strangest and most uncomfortable stories about provision in the whole Bible
> — and it's worth sitting with the discomfort, not rushing past it.

**[0:30–2:00] The text**

> For thus saith the Lord God of Israel, The barrel of meal shall not waste, neither
> shall the cruse of oil fail, until the day that the Lord sendeth rain upon the
> earth.

That's the promise. But look at what comes right before it in the full story.

**[2:00–5:00] The story**

Read 1 Kings 17:8–16 carefully. There's a famine across the whole region. Elijah,
himself a fugitive with a death sentence on his head, meets a widow gathering sticks
and asks her for water and bread. She tells him plainly: "I have not a cake, but an
handful of meal in a barrel, and a little oil in a cruse: and, behold, I am gathering
two sticks, that I may go in and dress it for me and my son, that we may eat it, and
die" (v. 12). This is not a woman with margin. This is a woman at the absolute end of
what she has. And Elijah's answer is not comforting in the way we might want it to be
— he doesn't say "don't worry, I'll find food elsewhere." He says: "make me thereof a
little cake first" (v. 13). Feed the stranger before you feed yourself and your
child. It is, on its face, an enormous ask.

She does it. And the flour and oil last for the entire duration of the famine (v.
14–16). But notice: the miracle didn't happen before her act of costly generosity. It
happened as a direct response to it. This is the same shape as the passage in 2
Kings 4 about Elisha and the widow's oil — she had to go get empty vessels from her
neighbors first, an act of faith-filled preparation, before the oil multiplied to
fill them.

**[5:00–6:30] The balance**

It would be easy — and wrong — to turn this into a formula: "give away your last
resource and God will multiply it." That's not what the text offers as a repeatable
transaction. What it shows is something narrower and, honestly, harder: sometimes
obedience looks like doing the thing that makes no sense by ordinary math, because a
word from God stands behind it. This widow wasn't told to give away her food to
anyone who asked her; she was responding to a specific word from a specific prophet
in a specific moment. The text isn't a blank check for reckless generosity. It's a
record of what obedience to a genuine word from God looked like, once, for one woman.

**[6:30–7:30] A moment of reflection**

Consider what it must have felt like to scrape out that last handful of meal, not
knowing yet whether it would run out the next morning or not. The text doesn't say
she felt confident. It says she obeyed, and then found out, day after day, that the
jar wasn't empty. Faith here isn't a feeling of certainty beforehand — it's the
willingness to act rightly and then discover, over time, that the provision holds.

**[7:30–8:15] Prayer**

> Lord, You fed Elijah by ravens, and this widow by flour that would not fail. I
> don't ask You for a formula I can repeat on demand — I ask for the same thing she
> had: a word I can trust, and the courage to obey it even when the math looks
> impossible. Amen.

**[8:15–8:30] Soft close**

> Next time: five loaves, two fish, and five thousand people — what actually happens
> when Jesus takes the very little that's offered to Him.

## Episode 6: "Five Loaves and the Lord Who Multiplies the Little We Have"
### Script (approx. 8:45)

**[0:00–0:30] Hook**

> A boy's packed lunch. Five thousand hungry people. And twelve baskets of leftovers
> at the end. This episode is about what Jesus actually did with the loaves — and
> what He didn't do.

**[0:30–2:00] The text**

> And he commanded the multitude to sit down on the grass, and took the five loaves,
> and the two fishes, and looking up to heaven, he blessed, and brake, and gave the
> loaves to his disciples, and the disciples to the multitude. And they did all eat,
> and were filled: and they took up of the fragments that remained twelve baskets
> full.

**[2:00–5:00] The story**

Read the fuller account in Matthew 14:13–21 and John 6:1–13. This happens right after
Jesus hears that John the Baptist has been beheaded — He withdraws by boat to be
alone, and the crowds follow Him on foot anyway. By evening, the disciples want to
send everyone away to buy food in the villages. Jesus' response is almost startling:
"They need not depart; give ye them to eat" (Matthew 14:16). Give ye them to eat.
Not "watch Me provide it out of nothing while you stand back." The disciples are
handed responsibility they clearly don't think they can meet — Philip does the math
in John's account and says two hundred denarii wouldn't be enough (John 6:7). Andrew
finds a boy with five barley loaves and two small fish and says, almost
apologetically, "but what are they among so many?" (John 6:9). That small, insufficient
thing is exactly what Jesus takes, blesses, and multiplies. He didn't produce bread
from nothing. He took what was actually, physically offered — small as it was — and
made it enough, and more than enough.

**[5:00–6:30] The balance**

Notice the division of labor in the miracle itself: Jesus blessed and broke the bread;
the disciples distributed it. The multiplication was entirely God's work. The
organizing, seating the crowds in groups of fifty and a hundred (Mark 6:39–40),
walking among five thousand people handing out food — that part was very much human
labor, carried out by ordinary men, on ordinary tired feet, for what must have been
hours. Abundance, here, still required hands willing to do the unglamorous work of
distribution.

**[6:30–7:45] A moment of reflection**

Sit with Andrew's line for a moment: "what are they among so many?" That is very
often exactly how our own resources feel next to the size of the need in front of us
— laughably small, embarrassingly inadequate. The story doesn't ask us to pretend our
five loaves are actually plenty on their own. It asks us to bring them anyway, and
let the blessing happen to what's actually in our hands, rather than waiting until we
have something more impressive to offer.

**[7:45–8:30] Prayer**

> Lord Jesus, You who blessed five loaves to feed thousands — bless also the little
> I have, that it may be enough. Teach me not to withhold what feels too small to
> matter, but to bring it forward and let You do with it what only You can do. And
> give me the willingness to do the ordinary, tiring work of distribution once the
> blessing has happened. Amen.

**[8:30–8:45] Soft close**

> Coming up: a passage that flips this whole conversation around — what happens when
> we're the ones doing the giving, and how that giving comes back around.

## Episode 7: "He Who Sows Bountifully Reaps Bountifully"
### Script (approx. 8:20)

**[0:00–0:30] Hook**

> Every episode so far has been about receiving. This one is about giving — and about
> a promise attached to it that's easy to misread as a transaction, when it's
> actually describing a posture of the heart.

**[0:30–2:00] The text**

> But this I say, He which soweth sparingly shall reap also sparingly; and he which
> soweth bountifully shall reap also bountifully. Every man according as he purposeth
> in his heart, so let him give; not grudgingly, or of necessity: for God loveth a
> cheerful giver. And God is able to make all grace abound toward you; that ye,
> always having all sufficiency in all things, may abound to every good work.

**[2:00–4:30] The story**

This passage comes from Paul's appeal to the church in Corinth to contribute to a
relief collection for believers suffering famine in Jerusalem (read 2 Corinthians
8–9 together for the full context). This is not abstract spiritual advice — it's Paul
asking real people to give real money to help other real people they'd likely never
meet. And notice how carefully he frames it: "not grudgingly, or of necessity" (v. 7).
He's explicitly not trying to guilt or pressure anyone. A few verses earlier, in
chapter 8, he holds up the example of the Macedonian churches, who gave "beyond their
power" even though they themselves were in "deep poverty" (8:2–3) — not because Paul
demanded it, but out of what he calls their own free choice.

**[4:30–6:00] The balance**

The phrase "may abound to every good work" at the end of verse 8 is the key that
unlocks the whole passage, and it's often skipped past. The abundance being promised
here isn't described as ending in a bigger personal stockpile — it's described as
resourcing more giving. Read on to verse 11: "Being enriched in every thing to all
bountifulness, which causeth through us thanksgiving to God." The purpose clause
matters. This isn't "give a little, get a lot back for yourself, and stop there." It's
closer to a description of a cycle: generosity resourced by God's grace, producing
more capacity for generosity, producing more thanksgiving. It's not a vending machine.
It's an ecosystem.

**[6:00–7:00] A moment of reflection**

Sit for a moment with "not grudgingly, or of necessity." Two very different kinds of
giving get ruled out here at once: giving out of guilt, and giving out of
calculation — giving because you feel you have to, and giving because you're
expecting a specific return. What's left, once both of those are set aside, is
something much simpler: giving because your heart has actually decided to. That's a
much harder thing to manufacture on demand than either guilt or a formula, and maybe
that's the point.

**[7:00–8:00] Prayer**

> Lord, teach me to give the way Paul describes — not grudgingly, not calculating a
> return, but because You have made my heart genuinely willing. Whatever You give me
> beyond what I need, let it flow toward more giving, not just toward a bigger pile
> for myself. Make me, as Paul says, rich toward every good work. Amen.

**[8:00–8:20] Soft close**

> One episode left in this series — and it's the one that balances everything we've
> said so far. A hard word from Paul about work, sitting right alongside every
> promise of provision we've read.

## Episode 8: "If Any Would Not Work, Neither Should He Eat"
### Script (approx. 8:40)

**[0:00–0:30] Hook**

> This is the last episode in the series, and it's the most important corrective in
> the whole set. If you've only watched this one, watch the other seven too — because
> this verse only makes sense standing next to all the promises that came before it.

**[0:30–2:00] The text**

> For even when we were with you, this we commanded you, that if any would not work,
> neither should he eat. For we hear that there are some which walk among you
> disorderly, working not at all, but are busybodies. Now them that are such we
> command and exhort by our Lord Jesus Christ, that with quietness they work, and eat
> their own bread.

**[2:00–4:30] The story**

Paul is writing to the church in Thessalonica, where — it seems — some believers had
concluded, based on a misunderstanding about the timing of Christ's return, that there
was no longer any point in ordinary work (read the wider context in 1 Thessalonians
4:11–12 and 2 Thessalonians 2:1–2, where Paul specifically corrects confusion about
"the day of the Lord" having already come). Some people had apparently stopped
working entirely and had, in Paul's words, become "busybodies" instead — meddling in
other people's business rather than tending to their own. Paul's response is blunt,
almost startlingly so for a man who elsewhere writes so warmly about grace: "if any
would not work, neither should he eat." He even points to his own example a few
verses earlier (3:7–9): he and his companions worked "night and day" while they were
there, specifically so as not to be a burden, even though as apostles they had every
right to be supported by the church.

**[4:30–6:30] The balance**

This is the verse that has to sit alongside every single promise in this series.
Psalm 23's Shepherd, the manna, the widow's oil, the five loaves — none of them were
ever meant to be read as "so you don't need to work anymore." Isaac still sowed.
Peter still fished all night before the miraculous catch. The widow still had to
gather sticks and bake the cake. Manna still had to be picked up off the ground every
single morning. There isn't a single story in this whole series where God's provision
arrives and human effort simply stops. What changes, story after story, isn't
whether people work — it's whether they work in anxious self-reliance or in
trusting, unhurried dependence on God alongside their labor.

**[6:30–7:45] A moment of reflection**

There's real compassion underneath this hard word, if you look closely. Paul isn't
condemning people who cannot work due to illness, disability, or lack of opportunity
— his target is specifically people who are capable and choosing idleness, dressed up
as spiritual conviction. It's worth being honest that this verse has sometimes been
twisted to shame people going through genuine hardship, unemployment, or disability —
that is a misuse of the text, not its intent. The point isn't to add guilt to
someone already struggling to find work. The point is to correct a specific,
different problem: people quitting the ordinary business of living, under cover of
spiritual language, when they were fully capable of continuing it.

**[7:45–8:30] Prayer**

> Lord, thank You for every promise of provision in this series — and thank You,
> too, for this word that keeps them honest. Give me work to do, and give me the
> willingness to do it with my whole heart, not looking for an excuse to set it down.
> And where I truly cannot work, or cannot find it despite trying, meet me there too
> — not with shame, but with the same care You showed Elijah, the widow, and Peter.
> Amen.

**[8:30–8:40] Soft close**

> That's the series — eight episodes, eight passages, one thread running through all
> of them: trust God, and keep working. Thank you for watching all the way through.

## Production notes

- Each script above runs 8:15–8:45 as written; actual on-camera pacing (pauses for
  reading Scripture on screen, B-roll, etc.) will likely bring most episodes past the
  9-minute mark, which comfortably clears the 8-minute floor.
- Keep the same opening/closing musical cue across all eight episodes for series
  continuity.
- If subtitles or on-screen text are used for the Scripture passages, hold each verse
  on screen long enough to be read aloud twice — this naturally extends runtime while
  keeping the pacing meditative rather than rushed.
- Avoid comment-bait calls to action ("type X and receive Y") — this series is
  specifically a corrective to that pattern.
